8-Ball Rogue

A mobile roguelike where billiard physics meets roguelike progression

Overview My Role Gallery Technical
Mobile Roguelike Team Project Android Unity + C#

Overview

In 8-Ball Rogue you play as a white billiard ball, moving from room to room filled with dangerous billiard balls that try to defeat you. Sinking all enemies in a room opens a portal where you can select one of three random upgrades and enter the next room. After every five rooms you reach a new level with different rooms, a different visual style, different obstacles, and stronger enemies. You earn coins after every room, which you can use in the main menu to buy permanent upgrades or unlock new abilities that can be found during a run.

There are four visually different levels the player can reach, which means a full run sees 20 rooms. Each room is pulled from a level-specific pool and contains random enemies in fixed positions, with random obstacles in fixed layouts.

Developing for mobile was a new experience for the team, because screens and UI elements had to account for different screen sizes. Because the player controls the main ball by dragging and shooting, UI elements that activate specific abilities had to be implemented with consideration for accidental activations.

Upgrades come in two flavours: active abilities and passive upgrades. Active abilities are triggered via on-screen buttons during gameplay. Explosion, for example, creates a large blast that pushes all nearby enemies away—great for creating chaos and sinking enemies at range. Freeze instantly stops the player's momentum, letting them line up another shot immediately, which opens up creative trick-shot plays. Passive upgrades include things like increased maximum speed—more speed means more impact force, which means more fun—or a Shield that blocks one enemy collision or projectile while standing still, giving the player a safety net against a single mistake.

Enemies are billiard balls with distinct behaviours, each using an ability on cooldown. Particle effects signal how close an enemy is to activating. The Dash enemy is a heavier ball that charges at the player periodically. The Projectile enemy fires a fast, powerful shot on cooldown. The Magic Projectile enemy is lighter and launches a slow projectile that pushes the player toward the nearest hole on contact. The Black Hole enemy is the most dangerous—it spawns a temporary black hole that functions like a regular pocket, instantly removing a life if the player falls in.

It was our vision as a team to create a fun, casual gameplay experience by utilizing billiard elements in an interesting way and expanding them with creative, imaginative abilities and possibilities.

My Role: Systems Architecture

While this was a team effort, I owned the foundational systems that made 8-Ball Rogue scalable and easy to balance. Specifically: tile systems, level/room generation and management, and the centralized enemy spawn and difficulty configuration.

Core Responsibility

Design and implement flexible systems for level layout, enemy spawning, and difficulty scaling—enabling designers to create and balance content without touching code.

Tile System Architecture

Rooms are built on a grid using custom rule tiles, where each tile type carries specific properties: walls, holes, and potential obstacle spawn points are all placed by designers directly in the Unity editor. However, the tile layer only defines the layout. When a room loads, obstacles are instantiated as prefabs on a random selection of marked spawn tiles, and hole tiles get replaced by collider-equipped prefabs that provide the gameplay functionality. This two-layer approach—tiles for layout, prefabs for behavior—was necessary because holes needed reliable world-space positions for collision detection, which proved difficult to achieve with tiles alone.

This hybrid approach made the system flexible enough to support four visually distinct levels. The same rule tiles are reused across all levels, but their sprites swap out based on the configured visual style, meaning new levels could be themed without rebuilding room layouts from scratch. That said, the tile-based foundation did introduce some friction—particularly around world-position accuracy—which taught us valuable lessons about when tiles are the right abstraction and when prefab-first designs might be simpler.

Level & Room System

The game uses a hybrid approach: room shapes—walls, holes, obstacle spots, and numbered enemy positions—are hand-crafted by designers in the Unity editor. But what fills those rooms is controlled by one central ScriptableObject configuration. This single config defines the number of levels, their names, how many rooms each level contains, the min and max counts for enemies and obstacles, and the pools from which rooms, enemies, and obstacles are drawn per level. When a room loads, it pulls a random room layout from the current level's pool and populates it with a random selection of enemies and obstacles within the configured bounds.

For designers, adding a new room meant building the layout in the editor with the established tile types, then adding it to the relevant level pool in the central config. The visual style for each level is also configured centrally—identical rule tiles receive different sprites depending on the level, applied automatically at the start of each level. This kept the workflow simple: designers focused on interesting layouts, while the system handled variety and visual theming.

Enemy Spawn & Difficulty System

Enemy spawning is driven entirely by the central ScriptableObject configuration. Each level defines its own enemy pool—a set of enemy types that can appear—along with minimum and maximum spawn counts. When a room loads, the system reads the numbered enemy positions from the hand-crafted layout, then fills a random subset of those positions with enemies drawn from the current level's pool, respecting the configured count bounds.

Difficulty scales naturally through the level structure: later levels introduce stronger enemy types into their pools and increase the spawn count ranges. Within a level, the randomized selection ensures variety while the configured min/max values keep the challenge within a tunable range. Because all of this lives in a single config file, balancing was a matter of adjusting numbers rather than touching code—designers could experiment with enemy compositions and spawn densities and immediately see the results in-game.

Visual Effects & UI

Beyond core systems, I was also responsible for the player and enemy visual effects. Each enemy's cooldown ability is telegraphed through particle systems that ramp up as the ability nears activation, giving the player a readable visual cue to react to. I also contributed to parts of the mobile UI, where the main challenge was designing touch-friendly ability buttons that wouldn't trigger accidentally during the drag-to-shoot input.

Data-Driven Design

A core principle behind the architecture was keeping game data out of code. One central ScriptableObject acts as the single source of truth for the entire run structure: level count, room pools, enemy pools, obstacle pools, spawn ranges, and visual styles. This meant that adding a new level or rebalancing enemy counts never required a code change, just editing the config asset in the Unity inspector. The same pattern applied to individual enemy and obstacle definitions, which were also ScriptableObjects with their own configurable properties. This data-driven approach was especially valuable in a team of five, where not everyone was comfortable working directly in code.

Systems Design Philosophy

The guiding principle was centralization and configurability. Rather than spreading game rules across dozens of scripts, one ScriptableObject config controls the entire run structure: levels, rooms, enemies, obstacles, and visual styles. This made it possible for any team member to tweak game balance, add content, or adjust the progression without writing code. The architecture prioritized fast iteration on mobile, where build times and testing cycles are already slower than desktop development.

Tile System Implementation

Rooms use Unity's Rule Tile system with custom tile types that carry gameplay-relevant properties. Walls and floor tiles define the room boundary and playable area. Hole tiles and obstacle-spawn tiles mark positions that the room loader processes at runtime: holes get replaced with collider prefabs that handle sinking logic, and obstacle spots receive randomly selected obstacle prefabs from the level's pool. The rule tile setup also enables the visual style system—the same tile rules apply across all levels, but their sprite sets swap based on the level's configured theme.

Level Generation & Room Management

Each level maintains a pool of hand-crafted room layouts. When the player enters a new room, the system selects a random layout from the current level's pool and instantiates it. The room's numbered enemy positions and obstacle spots are then populated based on the central config's min/max ranges and the level's enemy and obstacle pools. This hybrid approach gives designers full control over spatial design while the randomization layer provides replayability—no two runs feel identical even with the same room layouts.

Enemy Spawning & Configuration

The spawn system reads from per-level configurations that define which enemy types are available and how many can appear. Each room layout contains numbered spawn positions placed by designers. At load time, the system selects a count within the configured range and fills that many positions with enemies randomly drawn from the level's pool. Adding a new enemy type is straightforward: create the enemy prefab with its behavior script, then add it to the relevant level pools in the central config. Designers control the "what" and "how many"—the code handles the "where" and "when."

Difficulty Scaling

Difficulty progresses through the level structure itself. Each level defines its own enemy pool with progressively stronger enemy types, and the spawn count ranges increase as levels advance. Within a level, the randomized enemy selection from the pool provides natural variation in challenge. This config-driven approach made balancing a matter of adjusting pool compositions and count ranges rather than tuning algorithms—practical for a small team that needed to iterate quickly through playtesting.

Mobile Optimization

Billiard physics on Android required careful attention. The collision-heavy gameplay—multiple balls bouncing, rolling, and sinking simultaneously—put pressure on the physics engine. Tuning physics timesteps, collider precision, and rigidbody sleep thresholds was necessary to maintain consistent performance across a range of Android devices. The prefab-over-tile approach for holes and obstacles also helped here, as it gave us tighter control over collider shapes and physics interactions compared to tile-based colliders.

Lessons & Future Iterations

The biggest lesson was about team communication. With five people at varying experience levels, art and sound assets went through numerous reworks, and project goals needed multiple reevaluations throughout development. On the technical side, the tile-based room foundation introduced friction—particularly around world-position accuracy for gameplay objects—that a prefab-first approach might have avoided. The centralized ScriptableObject config, however, proved its value: it became the team's shared language for discussing balance and content, and it made late-stage adjustments far less painful than they would have been otherwise.

Scalable Systems in Action

8-Ball Rogue proved that well-architected systems enable fast iteration, reliable balancing, and designer empowerment. The tile, room, and difficulty systems I built became the foundation for confidently adding new content throughout development. That's the kind of systems thinking I bring to every project.
